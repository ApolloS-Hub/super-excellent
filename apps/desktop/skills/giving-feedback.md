# Giving Feedback

## Overview
Most feedback fails not because of content but because of timing, framing, or channel. Feedback given well is a gift; feedback given poorly is a wound. The core principle: care personally, challenge directly. Neither alone works.

## When to Use
- Reviewing someone's work / PR / design / writing
- Noticing a repeated behavior pattern worth naming
- Preparing for a performance review or 1:1
- Being asked for input on a draft
- Course-correcting early on a small issue before it grows

## Process

### 1. Before giving — check your motivation
- Is this for their growth, or for my frustration?
- Would I still give this feedback if it weren't convenient for me right now?
- Am I the right person to give this?

If the answer to any is shaky, delay or redirect.

### 2. Use SBI — Situation, Behavior, Impact
- **Situation**: when / where specifically
- **Behavior**: what I observed (not interpretation)
- **Impact**: what happened as a result

Example (weak):
> "You were kind of dismissive in the meeting"

Example (strong with SBI):
> "In Tuesday's roadmap review (S), when Priya brought up the security concern, you said 'we'll deal with that later' and moved the slide (B). Priya didn't raise another concern for the rest of the meeting, and the room got quieter (I)."

SBI is observable, specific, and hard to argue with.

### 3. Balance direct + caring (Kim Scott's Radical Candor)
Avoid the three failure modes:
- **Ruinous empathy**: caring but vague → feels nice, changes nothing
- **Obnoxious aggression**: direct but uncaring → hurts, creates resistance
- **Manipulative insincerity**: neither direct nor caring → the worst

Target: "I care about your growth AND here's the specific thing to change."

### 4. Make it a conversation, not a verdict
After giving the feedback:
- Pause. Let them respond.
- Ask: "How does this land for you?" or "What did I miss?"
- Listen without rebutting. Paraphrase back what you heard.

Feedback they don't accept won't change anything. Understanding their view lets you meet them where they are.

### 5. Close with forward motion
Don't end on the problem. Agree on:
- What they'll try differently
- What support they need from you
- When you'll check in

### 6. For positive feedback — be specific too
"Great job" is background noise. Strong praise uses SBI too:
> "Your async update on Friday (S) was structured exactly right: decisions + blockers + asks (B). The engineering team responded faster than usual (I). Keep doing that."

Specific praise reinforces the *behavior*, not just makes them feel good.

### 7. Frequency beats intensity
Small, frequent feedback (weekly 1:1s, same-day PR comments) outperforms big yearly reviews. If you're saving something for the review, it's stale by the time you deliver it.

## Rationalizations

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "They're too junior to hear this" | They're more resilient than you think; vagueness hurts them more |
| "I'll wait until the next 1:1" | If it's worth saying, it's worth saying within 48h |
| "I don't want to damage the relationship" | Withholding honest feedback damages it slower but deeper |
| "They should figure it out themselves" | Sometimes. But if they haven't in 3 months, they need input |
| "I'm not their manager" | Peer feedback is often the most valuable (less politicized) |

## Red Flags
- You're about to say "you always…" or "you never…" → too sweeping
- You're labeling the person, not the behavior ("you're lazy")
- You've rehearsed the speech but haven't invited dialogue
- You're giving feedback in front of other people (unless deliberately public)
- You're giving 20 items at once — prioritize 2-3 most important
- You're giving feedback by email for something emotionally charged

## Verification
- [ ] SBI structure used (specific situation + observable behavior + clear impact)
- [ ] Feedback is about behavior, not character / identity
- [ ] Invited their perspective after giving
- [ ] Agreed on a concrete next step
- [ ] If critical: given privately, not in a group
- [ ] Delivered within 48h of the event, when possible
