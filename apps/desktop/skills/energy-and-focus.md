# Energy and Focus Management

## Overview
Time is not the scarcest resource — focused time is. Most people have 8-10 hours in their day but only 2-4 hours of peak cognitive energy. The goal isn't to do more; it's to put your best energy on your most important work.

## When to Use
- Feeling busy but not making progress on what matters
- Constantly reactive — responding to messages, never deep work
- Finding evenings / weekends contaminated with work anxiety
- Mornings spent on email; afternoons too drained for real work
- Calendar fully booked but meaningful output low

## Process

### 1. Audit the last week
Before changing anything, see the data. For one week, log each hour's primary activity. Tag each hour as:
- **Deep** — focused on one hard problem (writing, coding, strategy)
- **Shallow** — email, status updates, routine replies, admin
- **Collaborative** — meetings, 1:1s, pairing
- **Recovery** — breaks, walks, exercise (not scrolling)
- **Leaked** — Slack/social/news during "work" time

Most people discover they have 1-3 deep hours per day, not 8. That's normal. The goal is to not waste them.

### 2. Block — don't reserve, protect
Two types of calendar events:
- **Deep work blocks**: 90-120 min, mornings, phone off, Slack paused, one problem
- **Shallow blocks**: 30-60 min for email/admin, batched once or twice a day

If it's not on the calendar, it won't happen. If it's on the calendar but unprotected (people book over it), it won't happen either.

### 3. The "one big thing" rule
Every morning, choose ONE thing that, if completed, will make the day a win. That's your deep-work target. Everything else is optional.

If you can't name one thing, that's a planning problem, not a work problem.

### 4. Respect your chronotype
- **Morning person?** Do deep work 7-10am. Protect that time like oxygen.
- **Evening person?** Do shallow / meetings in the morning, deep work after 5pm.

Don't fight your biology. Schedule meetings in your *low-energy* hours.

### 5. Mid-day transition
Between deep work sessions, take a real break:
- 10-min walk (outside > inside > hallway)
- No phone, no screen
- Water, food if needed

Studies: 5-15 min of low-stimulation break resets cognitive function. Scrolling does not.

### 6. End the day deliberately
- Last 10 min: tomorrow's "one big thing" written down
- Close laptop. Physical signal: walk away from the desk
- If you can't stop thinking about work, write the thought down — you'll trust it's saved

### 7. Weekly reset — Friday afternoon
- Review the week: what moved? what didn't?
- Plan next week's 3-5 big rocks
- Clear open loops: unreplied messages, lingering TODOs, unresolved emails

## Rationalizations

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "I work better under pressure" | Maybe. But chronic pressure → burnout, not productivity |
| "Meetings are my work" | If 80% of your time is meetings, your work isn't happening |
| "I'll catch up on weekends" | Weekend work is tax on next week's energy. Break even at best |
| "I can multitask" | You can't. Every context switch costs 15-25 min of refocus |
| "I don't have time for breaks" | Breaks pay back more than they cost. Always |

## Red Flags
- Calendar fully booked 9-5
- Email checked more than 5-6 times per day
- Meetings with no prep time between them
- "Busy" feels good because it silences anxiety
- Can't recall what you actually shipped this week
- Sleep under 7h for multiple nights in a row

## Verification
- [ ] Past week audited (deep / shallow / meetings / leaked)
- [ ] At least 2 × 90-min deep work blocks scheduled weekly
- [ ] "One big thing" written down each morning
- [ ] Meetings batched (not scattered)
- [ ] Phone / Slack has scheduled "do not disturb" hours
- [ ] Friday reset done this week
