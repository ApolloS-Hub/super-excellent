# Saying No

## Overview
Every yes is a no to something else. People who can't say no don't have more capacity — they have worse work, quieter resentment, and eventual burnout. Saying no well is a professional skill, not a personality trait.

## When to Use
- Someone senior asks for work you can't / shouldn't take
- A peer asks for help during your focus time
- A recruiter, vendor, or external contact requests your time
- Scope creep on an existing project
- Being added to a meeting that doesn't need you
- An invitation that isn't aligned with your priorities

## Process

### 1. Buy a moment if you need one
You don't owe instant answers. Defer:
- "Let me check my week and get back to you by EOD."
- "Good question — I need to think about this. Circle back tomorrow?"

Instant yeses you regret are the main source of overload. Instant thoughtful pauses are rare and respected.

### 2. Structure the no — 3 parts

**Part 1: The clean no (one sentence)**
- ✅ "I can't take this on."
- ✅ "I'm going to pass on this one."
- ❌ "Well, I'd love to but my schedule is kind of crazy right now and I just don't think I can…" (the hedge invites pressure to negotiate)

**Part 2: The reason (brief, true)**
- "I'm on a launch deadline through end of month."
- "This isn't in my focus area right now."
- "My plate is full with [X, Y]."

Don't over-explain. One sentence max. Over-explaining signals guilt, and guilt invites pushback.

**Part 3: The alternative (optional but powerful)**
- "I can't do it, but [Alice] would be a great fit."
- "Not this quarter, but put it on my list for next."
- "I can't write the whole doc, but I'll review a draft in 30 min."

A partial offer or pointer preserves the relationship and often solves the underlying need.

### 3. For senior requests — frame as trade-off, not refusal
When saying no to someone with more authority, make visible the trade-off:
> "Happy to take this on, but it means X (current priority) slips by 2 weeks. Which do you want?"

They'll usually un-request. If they don't, you now have explicit approval to deprioritize X.

### 4. Don't apologize excessively
- ❌ "So sorry, I'm really sorry, I feel awful, I wish I could…"
- ✅ "I can't. Thanks for asking."

One brief acknowledgment of empathy is plenty. Excessive apology makes the other person feel guilty for asking, which poisons the relationship more than a clean no would.

### 5. For repeated asks — escalate the explanation
First no: polite, brief
Second no (same person, same ask): "As I mentioned, I can't take this on. Let's close this one."
Third no: direct + name the pattern: "I've said no twice. Please don't keep asking."

### 6. Don't say no and then sneak-yes
The worst outcome: say no publicly, then quietly do it anyway because of guilt. Destroys your credibility AND still takes your time. Either do it (explicit yes) or don't (explicit no).

## Rationalizations

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "I can't say no to my boss" | You can say "yes AND here's the trade-off". That IS saying no to the current priority |
| "I'll disappoint them" | Brief disappointment now < chronic mediocre work for them |
| "Saying no makes me look lazy" | Saying yes to everything makes you look overcommitted and unreliable |
| "It won't take that long" | It will. It always does. Plus the context switch |
| "If I say no, they won't ask me again" | If it's a good request and you're a good fit, they'll ask when it actually fits |

## Red Flags
- You're explaining for 3+ sentences why you can't
- You're promising "next time" without a date
- You're saying yes to avoid the conversation, planning to quietly drop it
- You're counter-offering scope you also don't have time for
- "I'll try my best" (that's a soft yes, not a no)
- Saying yes to one person at the cost of a commitment to another

## Verification
- [ ] Refusal fits in one sentence (before optional reason/alternative)
- [ ] Reason is true and brief (no over-explaining)
- [ ] No excessive apology language
- [ ] If senior: trade-off made visible
- [ ] Not quietly doing the thing anyway
- [ ] If repeat asker: pattern named after 2-3 nos
