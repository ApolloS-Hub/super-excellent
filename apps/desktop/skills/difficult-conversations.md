# Difficult Conversations

## Overview
Avoided conversations compound. The cost of saying nothing is usually higher than the cost of saying it carefully. This skill helps structure difficult messages so they land as caring, not as attacks.

## When to Use
- Giving critical feedback to a peer or report
- Saying no to a request from a senior stakeholder
- Raising a concern about someone's behavior or work
- Declining an opportunity without burning a bridge
- De-escalating a conflict already in flight

## Process

### 1. Clarify your own intent first
Write down — for yourself only — answers to:
- What outcome do I actually want from this conversation?
- What's my honest emotional state? (frustration / fear / disappointment)
- Am I optimizing for the relationship, the work, or both?

If your intent is "to vent" or "to win", stop. That's a different conversation.

### 2. Use the SBI frame for feedback
**Situation → Behavior → Impact**, not character judgment:
- ❌ "You're being negative in meetings"
- ✅ "In yesterday's standup (S), when Alice proposed the API redesign you said 'that'll never work' before she finished (B). She stopped proposing ideas for the rest of the meeting (I)."

Keep it specific, recent, and observable.

### 3. Lead with intent + invitation
Open the conversation by naming what you're doing and creating an exit:
- "I want to raise something with you because I care about how we work together."
- "Can I share something that's been on my mind? Feel free to push back."

### 4. For "no" responses — the full-sentence no
- ❌ "I don't think I can get to that this week…" (hedge invites pressure)
- ✅ "I'm not going to take this on. Here's why, and here's what I can do instead."

Three-part structure: **clear no → reason → alternative**.

### 5. Listen before defending
When they respond, your job is to understand their reaction — not rebut it. Ask:
- "What did I miss?"
- "How does this land for you?"

Paraphrase back what you heard before replying.

### 6. End with a small commitment
Don't let the conversation dissolve. End with: "Here's what I'll do differently / Here's what I'm asking from you / Let's check in on this Friday."

## Rationalizations

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "They should already know" | They don't. Say it explicitly |
| "I'll tell them over email" | Hard feedback in writing reads 5x harsher than spoken |
| "I'll soften it by adding compliments" | Praise sandwich dilutes both parts. Be direct |
| "This isn't the right time" | There's never a right time. There's 'now' or 'avoided' |
| "I'll let HR handle it" | If you have a direct relationship, you own it first |

## Red Flags
- You're drafting the message when angry — wait 24h
- You keep rehearsing but not sending
- You're labeling the person ("toxic", "lazy") instead of the behavior
- You're looking for a win rather than a resolution
- The stakes are too high for the channel (firing via Slack = wrong)

## Verification
- [ ] Your intent is clear and relational, not adversarial
- [ ] Feedback uses SBI (situation + behavior + impact), not labels
- [ ] If it's a "no", the no is in the first sentence
- [ ] You've left room for their response before closing
- [ ] You've agreed on a concrete next step or check-in
