# Written Communication

## Overview
Most business writing is bloated, hedged, and buried. The reader bails in the first two sentences. This skill produces writing that respects the reader's time — front-loaded, specific, and action-oriented.

## When to Use
- Drafting a short email or Slack message
- Writing a status update
- Sending an ask / request
- Announcing a decision or change
- Summarizing a long document for others

## Process

### 1. Start with BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front
First sentence: the outcome, decision, or ask. Everything else is supporting detail.
- ❌ "I wanted to reach out about the Q4 planning doc that we've been working on…"
- ✅ "I need your sign-off on the Q4 plan by Friday. Link below."

### 2. Answer the reader's 4 questions
Every business message should quickly answer:
- **What** is this about?
- **Why** am I reading it?
- **What** do I need to do? (the ask)
- **By when**?

If any answer isn't clear in the first 30 seconds, rewrite.

### 3. Kill hedges and filler
Strike on sight:
- "I just wanted to…" → delete "just wanted to"
- "I think maybe we could perhaps…" → "I recommend we…"
- "As you know…" → they know or they don't; either way, skip it
- "I hope this email finds you well" → replace with the actual point

### 4. One idea per paragraph
Break dense text. If a paragraph has three ideas, that's three paragraphs.

### 5. Choose the format
- **One thing, no discussion needed** → Slack / DM
- **Decision with 2-3 options** → async doc + comments
- **Announcement to 5+ people** → email with clear subject
- **Sensitive or complex** → verbal or video, written summary after

### 6. Subject lines that earn the open
- ❌ "Quick question"
- ❌ "Re: Re: Re: Update"
- ✅ "Decision needed by Fri: Q4 scope"
- ✅ "FYI only: launch moved to 3/15"

Lead the subject with a tag: `Decision needed`, `FYI`, `Action required`, `Blocker`.

### 7. Close with a clear ask
Last line should state what happens next:
- "Can you reply by Thursday with your decision?"
- "I'll assume we're a go unless I hear otherwise by 5pm."
- "No reply needed — just wanted you in the loop."

## Rationalizations

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "Being direct seems rude" | Being vague wastes more time and respects people less |
| "I need to explain all the context first" | Explain only what's needed to act; link the rest |
| "Longer emails show I've thought about it" | Shorter emails show you respect the reader |
| "I'll soften the ask with 'maybe'" | Vague asks get vague answers or are ignored |
| "Sending it now even though I'm not sure" | Better a delayed email than a confusing one |

## Red Flags
- Subject line is "Update" / "Question" / "Quick one"
- First sentence is small talk
- Three paragraphs of context before the ask
- Multiple asks bundled together
- No deadline on action items
- Using "we" when you mean "you" (ownership laundering)

## Verification
- [ ] BLUF in first sentence
- [ ] Reader can answer "what do I do?" within 30s
- [ ] No hedges, filler, or "just wanted to"
- [ ] Subject line tags the type of message
- [ ] Ask has a deadline (or explicitly says no reply needed)
- [ ] Length is *as short as possible*, not "complete"
